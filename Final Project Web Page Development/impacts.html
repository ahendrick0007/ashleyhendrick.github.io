Impacts of Tornadoes

What Happens During a Tornado

A tornado is a rapidly rotating column of air that extends from a thunderstorm to the ground. During a tornado, violent winds spiral around a central axis, causing widespread destruction along its path.

Tornadoes can form with little warning and move unpredictably, making them extremely dangerous natural disasters.

Types of Damages

Tornadoes can cause various types of damages, including:

  • Structural Damage: Tornadoes can demolish buildings, homes, and infrastructure, resulting in total or partial destruction.
  • Debris Impact: Flying debris propelled by tornado winds can act as projectiles, causing additional damage to structures and posing a significant threat to human safety.
  • Flooding: Tornadoes can disrupt drainage systems and cause flash floods, exacerbating damages and hazards.
  • Vegetation Destruction: Tornadoes can uproot trees, destroy crops, and strip vegetation, impacting agriculture and ecosystems.

Enhanced Fujita (EF) Scale

The intensity of tornadoes is categorized using the Enhanced Fujita (EF) scale, which rates tornadoes based on the estimated wind speeds and resulting damages:

  • EF0 - Weak: Wind speeds 65-85 mph; light damage, such as broken tree branches and minor roof damage.
  • EF1 - Moderate: Wind speeds 86-110 mph; moderate damage, including uprooted trees and significant roof damage.
  • EF2 - Strong: Wind speeds 111-135 mph; considerable damage, with roofs torn off buildings and mobile homes overturned.
  • EF3 - Severe: Wind speeds 136-165 mph; severe damage, with well-built homes completely destroyed and large trees uprooted.
  • EF4 - Devastating: Wind speeds 166-200 mph; devastating damage, including entire neighborhoods leveled and cars thrown considerable distances.
  • EF5 - Incredible: Wind speeds over 200 mph; catastrophic damage, with strong frame houses leveled to the ground and large objects become airborne missiles.
